Bill Crouse, Sr.

William Crouse Sr. is a member of the Seneca Nation of Indians of the Hawk Clan. He is a faithkeeper, singer, and speaker of the Coldspring Longhouse.

As group leader, singer, lecturer, and dancer of the Allegany River Indian Dancers, Bill has travelled all over the U.S. and Canada. He has performed in Rome, Italy, and Wurtzburg, Germany as well. He has worked with the American Indian Dance Theater as a choreographer and consultant and was featured in their video Dances of a New Generation. His music recordings of Iroquois Social dance music, Smoke dance music and re-mix are a hit with young and old alike.

As a graphic artist his work is displayed at the Seneca Iroquois National Museum, the Iroquois Museum, Howes Cave N.Y., The Seneca Allegany Casino, and many private collectors. He has also illustrated many designs for Native Stitches and Seneca Language Publications.

Andrew Cashner

Andrew A. Cashner, PhD, is an assistant professor of music at the University of Rochester. A pianist, composer, and director, he also leads the worship and creative arts ministry at New Hope Free Methodist Church in Rochester.

Born in Richmond, Indiana to descendents of German and English settler-colonists, he studied piano performance at Lawrence University (BMus., 2003) and the New England Conservatory of Music (graduate study, 2004), then earned a Master of Sacred Music degree at the University of Notre Dame (2009) and the PhD in the history and theory of music from the University of Chicago (2015). He is the author of Hearing Faith: Music as Theology in the Spanish Empire (Leiden: Brill, 2020), a study of religious devotion through music seventeenth-century Mexico and Spain, along with several journal articles and music editions on related topics.

I became interested in this project out of a desire to find ways to recognize indigenous sovereignty over the lands my ancestors falsely claimed, and to promote indigenous voices and cultural expressions. I live in Rochester’s Nineteenth Ward, on land guaranteed by George Washington to the Seneca Nation under the Candandaigua Treaty of 1794. In my lifetime I hope to see the full restoration of the Seneca Nation's ancestral lands.
